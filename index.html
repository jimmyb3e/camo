a brief history of camouflage

Before the advent of long-range weapons, personal concealment in warfare was relatiely rare, as unit cohesion and and near-range recognition were paramount in the close-quarters combat characteristic of pre-Napoleonic wars. With the introduction of accurate rifles and their subsequent widespread use in the Napoleonic Wars and beyond, militaries across Europe began to introduce camouflage among select units.

Initially, camouflage uniforms were composed of one color, and by the outbreak of World War I, most western militaries (with France as a notable exception) had fielded various shades of drab colors, ranging from khaki to green to field grey. The introduction of artillery, aerial warfare, and machine guns again forced military leaders to innovate in order to better conceal their soldiers, and new, multi-color patterns began to see use prior to World War I's conclusion, the idea being to disrupt outlines of and provide concealment for both soldiers and equipment.

Multi-color patterns spread quickly during and soon after World War II, and by the 1980s, most major militaries had adopted a multi-color camouflage uniform. Camouflage patterns witnessed a third evolution at the dawn of the 21st century when Canada debuted the first "digital" pattern. Traditional "blob" patterns proved somewhat ineffective under low-light conditions, and this issue became more pronounced as more militaries embraced night vision equipment for nighttime operations. Digital patterns tested substantially better in low-light conditions, and under ordinary conditions, they raised detection and recognition times dramatically compared to those of traditional patterns. Many of the world's major militaries have since designed, tested, and fielded digital patterns. As warfare continues to evolve, so does camouflage. The definition of camouflage has changed in recent times, as it now encompasses both visual and electronic means of concealment.
